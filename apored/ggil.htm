Líneas de Investigación

REGULATION OF APOPTOSIS OF QUIESCENT CELLS BY THE CELL CYCLE REGULATORY KINASE CDK2.

       Apoptosis is a physiological mechanism by which pluricellular organisms get rid of damaged or useless cells. This is a biochemically active process that involves specialised biochemical pathways. Among these proteins are the Bcl2 family members that regulate the release of mitochondrial apoptotic mediators like Cytochrome C. The in vivo study of the Bcl2 family proteins using genetically modified mice has revealed that they are involved in the regulation of both cell death and cell division. Our main interest is the study of the biochemical connection between apoptosis and cell cycle regulation.

       We have found that activation of the protein kinase Cdk2, originally involved in cell cycle regulation, is necessary for apoptosis of non-dividing cells like T-lymphocytes and neurons. The biochemical basis of these apparently opposite actions of Cdk2 is its association with specific activating subunits called cyclins. Thus, during cell cycle regulation Cdk2 sequentially associates with Cyclins E and A while we have found that during apoptosis it is activated by a different protein. We have isolated a new cyclin (Cyclin 5) that is able to specifically bind and activate Cdk2 and Cdk1 and induces apoptosis when overexpressed in quiescent or cycling cells. Cyclin 5 is expressed at very low levels in tissues in an expression pattern strikingly similar to DNA damage signalling and repair proteins like Nbs1, ATM and ATR. Preliminary results show that Cyclin 5 mRNA is induced by apoptotic stimuli such as DNA damaging drugs or glucocorticoids. Its expression leads to perturbation of the DNA damage checkpoints, cell cycle progression and DNA repair mechanisms. We are currently studying the biochemical connection of Cyclin 5 activation with all these mechanisms and how the formation of Cyclin 5-Cdk2 complexes is regulated and couples with activation of apical caspases like Caspase-8.

       Given the fact that these regulatory mechanisms seem to be operational in quiescent cells (that are the vast majority of the cells of the adult organisms), we think that their study will lead to a better understanding of pathologies characterised by excessive death of postmitotic cells like the neurodegenerative diseases or, in case of insufficient cell death, may be part of the process that leads to neoplasic transformation.
